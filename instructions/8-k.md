# Event-Driven Investment Thesis Evaluation

These instructions define a **rapid, high-signal evaluation process** for investment theses when material events occur (via 8-K filings). The objective is to determine whether new information **immediately affects the validity of the thesis**.

## Purpose of an event-driven evaluation

An event-driven thesis review is an **early warning system**.

Its goals are to:

* Detect material changes as early as possible
* Identify whether any thesis pillar is **suddenly weakened or broken**
* Distinguish **signal from noise** in disclosures
* Decide whether **immediate action** is required

This process is intentionally **faster and more focused** than quarterly or annual reviews.

## When to run an event-driven evaluation

Run this process only when a **potentially material event** occurs.

Examples include:

* Earnings releases (8-K Item 2.02)
* Debt issuance or new obligations (2.03)
* Defaults or financial distress (2.04)
* Equity issuance or dilution (3.02)
* Acquisitions or divestitures (2.01)
* Material agreements or contract changes (1.01, 1.02)
* Auditor changes or restatements (4.01, 4.02)
* Leadership changes (5.02)
* Impairments or restructuring (2.05, 2.06)

Ignore routine, administrative, or clearly immaterial filings.

## Preparation (before reviewing the filing)

Re-read only the **relevant parts of the thesis**:

* Core thesis
* Key pillars
* Invalidation criteria

Explicitly ask:

* Which pillar(s) could this event affect?
* What type of event would break this thesis?

This step prevents overreaction to headlines or narrative framing.

## What to review in the filing

Focus only on **new, decision-relevant information**.

Key areas:

* What actually changed
* Economic impact (cash flow, leverage, dilution)
* Strategic implications (business model, positioning)
* Credibility signals (management behavior, governance, accounting)
* Timing and permanence of the change

Ignore:

* Boilerplate disclosures
* Previously known information
* Narrative language without new facts

## Classify the event

Before evaluating the thesis, classify the event:

* **Informational**: No impact on thesis
* **Incremental**: Minor change, monitor
* **Material**: Affects one or more pillars
* **Critical**: Directly challenges or breaks the thesis

This step prevents unnecessary analysis and reduces noise.

## Map the event to the thesis

For each affected pillar, ask:

* Does this event strengthen, weaken, or not affect the pillar?
* Is the impact temporary or structural?
* Does this change the probability of success?

Update only the pillars that are directly impacted.

## Invalidation check (non-negotiable)

Explicitly confirm:

* Did this event trigger any invalidation criterion?

If **yes**, the default action is **exit or material reduction**, unless there is clear, evidence-based justification otherwise.

Do not reinterpret the thesis to accommodate the event.

## Urgency assessment

Determine whether action is required:

* No action → Informational or incremental
* Monitor → Uncertain or developing situation
* Act soon → Material deterioration
* Act immediately → Invalidation or severe risk

This step ensures timely response to critical events.

## Allowed decisions

Each event evaluation must end with one clear decision:

1. **No Action**: Event does not affect the thesis
2. **Watch**: Monitor for follow-up developments
3. **Reduce**: Thesis weakened or risk increased
4. **Exit**: Thesis broken or invalidated

Avoid ambiguous outcomes without defined reasoning.

## One-paragraph conclusion (recommended)

Write a short summary capturing:

* What happened
* Why it matters (or does not)
* Which pillar(s) are affected
* The resulting action

Example:

"Company announced a significant debt-funded acquisition, increasing leverage beyond prior assumptions. Balance sheet pillar weakened but not invalidated. Monitor integration and cash flow impact. Reduce position size."

This creates a **decision log** for future reference.

## Key rules to follow

* Do not treat all 8-K filings as equally important
* Do not react to headlines without understanding the substance
* Do not delay action when invalidation criteria are triggered
* Do not over-weight single events unless they are structural
* Focus on changes, not disclosure volume

## One-sentence rule

*Event-driven evaluation asks: does this new information materially change the thesis right now?*
