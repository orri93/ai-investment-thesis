# Annual Investment Thesis Revalidation

These instructions define a **deep, structural review** of each investment thesis, typically performed after the annual report (10-K / annual report) is available. The goal is to decide whether the investment still deserves capital **looking forward**, not to justify past decisions.

## Purpose of an annual revalidation

An annual thesis review is a **full re-underwriting exercise**.

Its goals are to:

-	Reassess long-term assumptions
-	Evaluate management’s capital allocation over a full year
-	Review balance-sheet strength and resilience
-	Decide whether you would **buy the position again today** at current weights

This review is intentionally slower and more comprehensive than a quarterly check.

## Preparation (before reading the annual report)

Re-read the **entire original thesis**, including:

-	Core thesis
-	What must go right
-	Key risks
-	Invalidation criteria
-	Role in portfolio

Explicitly ask:

-	What assumptions does this thesis rely on?
-	Which assumptions matter most for long-term returns?
-	Which assumptions would cause a permanent loss of capital if wrong?

Write these down mentally or briefly to avoid hindsight bias.
 
## What to review in the annual report

Focus on **structural, multi-year indicators**, not quarterly noise.

Key areas:

-	Revenue mix and trend sustainability
-	Margin structure and operating leverage
-	Capital expenditures and return on invested capital
-	Free cash flow generation and uses of cash
-	Balance-sheet strength, debt, and liquidity
-	Management incentives and governance
-	Competitive positioning and moat durability
-	Regulatory, legal, or geopolitical exposure

Ignore:

-	One-year EPS volatility
-	Non-recurring accounting items unless structural
-	Market commentary or stock performance

## Re-test the thesis pillars

For each major thesis pillar, ask:

-	Is this pillar **stronger, unchanged, or weaker** than a year ago?
-	Has the timeline meaningfully changed?
-	Are returns becoming more or less dependent on favorable conditions?

Use directional judgments rather than precise scoring.\

## Invalidation review (non-negotiable)

Explicitly confirm:

-	Did any invalidation criterion trigger during the year?

If **yes**, the default action is **exit or material reduction**, unless there is compelling, evidence-based justification.

Do not introduce new assumptions to preserve a broken thesis.
 
## Portfolio-level reassessment

The annual review must consider **relative opportunity cost**.

Ask:

-	Would I allocate new capital to this position today?
-	Does its role in the portfolio still make sense?
-	Has its risk contribution increased or decreased?
-	Are there better risk-adjusted alternatives available?

This step prevents “legacy holdings” from persisting without justification.

## Allowed decisions

Each annual review must end with one clear decision:

1. **Increase / Add**: Thesis strengthened and opportunity attractive
2. **Maintain**: Thesis intact, position size appropriate
3. **Reduce**: Thesis intact but returns or risk profile less attractive
4. **Exit**: Thesis broken or capital better redeployed

Ambiguous outcomes usually signal an unresolved thesis.

## Written conclusion (strongly recommended)

Write a short summary capturing:

- What changed structurally over the year
- What assumptions were validated or weakened
- The final decision and rationale

Example:

"*Pipeline execution improved, free cash flow normalized, and balance sheet strengthened. Core thesis intact but growth slower than expected. Maintain position at reduced weight.*"

This creates a durable **investment decision record**.

## Key rules to follow

-	Do not anchor on original purchase price
-	Do not confuse cyclical weakness with structural decline
-	Do not rationalize sunk costs
-	Treat capital as fully mobile every year

## One-sentence rule

*The annual review answers one question: would I commit this capital again today for the next 5–10 years?*
